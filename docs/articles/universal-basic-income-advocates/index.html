Who are the advocates of Universal Basic Income?

List of advocates of universal basic income

The first modern, detailed mention of a system resembling universal basic income (UBI) is widely attributed to Thomas More in his book Utopia, published in 1516. In the book, one character argues against harsh punishments for theft, proposing instead to: "provide everyone with some means of livelihood, so that nobody is under the frightful necessity of becoming first a thief, and then a corpse". This early idea of publicly provided subsistence to prevent poverty and crime served as a foundational concept, though it still involved an expectation of work, unlike the modern definition of an unconditional basic income.

  • Johannes Ludovicus Vives (1526) ‚Äî First to outline a detailed, publicly administered minimum-income scheme. Conditional on willingness to work.
  • Thomas Paine (1797) ‚Äî In Agrarian Justice, proposes a universal endowment for all young adults and the elderly, funded by land-rent. Early form of social insurance.
  • Thomas Spence (1797) ‚Äî First to propose a truly unconditional, regular basic income for all community members, financed through communal land rents.
  • Joseph Charlier (1848) ‚Äî Further develops the idea by advocating socialised rent distribution as a universal income.

These thinkers laid the intellectual foundation for modern UBI debates, which continue today amid concerns about automation and AI.

Advocates for Universal Basic Income (UBI) span economists, tech leaders, politicians, and activists, including Guy Standing, Rutger Bregman, Andrew Yang, Jack Dorsey, Chris Hughes, Elon Musk, Richard Branson, and historical figures like philosopher Bertrand Russell, with proponents arguing it addresses poverty, inequality, and technological job displacement, while implementing it via trials in places like Stockton city, California.
